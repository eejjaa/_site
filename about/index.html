About

Self-portrait with bee entering nose.
Examining collected ants in Cameroon.

I am a PhD student in Biology at the University of Utah. I study collective behavior, cooperation, and the emergent properties of complex systems. Specifically, I am focused on the mathematical and neurological mechanisms by which foraging animals can integrate information to optimize their foraging, as individuals and as a group. I do this by building mathematical models, running simulated experiments, and performing real experiments, which involves gluing QR codes to bumblebees’ backs and filming their activity.

I am also involved in many side projects. I received a grant to study reproduction and adaptation in a wild-harvested edible mushroom endemic to Chile, and just returned from a trip there to collect some specimens and meet with indigenous harvesters. I did similar work in Cameroon in 2018. Before starting graduate school, I worked as a professional beekeeper in Texas for several years; In 2017, I got to combine several of my passions by traveling to Cuba as an advisor to a National Geographic project by a Cuban filmmaker (watch it here).

I have spent many years thinking about the relationship between society and the environment. Early in college, I trained as a journalist, inspired by the lucid and powerful words of writers like John Pilger and Nicholas Kristof. Advertising was also taught in the journalism school, and I found myself drawn to it - not because I wanted to sell things (I didn’t), but because I wished to understand what motivates people to act. After all, reporting is one thing - inciting change is another. I wanted to understand the fundamental elements that underpin the behavior of individuals, groups, and our society. To that end, I’ve become increasingly interested in the crossover between my science and society, a subject that I explore in my blog.
